# German, Italian, and International CV Norms

The same career history gets formatted differently depending on who's reading it. This guide covers what changes between a German Lebenslauf, an Italian CV, and an international English resume — for both university applications and jobs.

## The quick comparison

| | German Lebenslauf | Italian CV | International resume (US/UK style) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Length | 1–2 pages | 1–2 pages (or Europass) | 1 page early-career |
| Photo | Traditional, now optional | Common | Avoided (bias concerns) |
| Date of birth / nationality | Sometimes included | Commonly included | Omitted |
| Format | Tabular, reverse-chronological | Often Europass | Clean, achievement-led |
| GDPR consent line | Not required | Conventional | Not used |
| Cover letter | Expected (Anschreiben) | Common | Sometimes |

## The German Lebenslauf

German employers expect a concise, factual, tabular CV called a **Lebenslauf**, usually one to two pages and in reverse-chronological order. It's organised into clear blocks: personal details, work experience, education, skills, and languages. Keep the prose out of it — the detail belongs in the cover letter.

A **photo** at the top is a long-standing German convention. It's becoming optional and many employers no longer expect it, so use your judgment, but it's still common to include a professional headshot.

The **Anschreiben** (cover letter) carries real weight in Germany. It should be tailored to the specific role, explain why you fit, and be written in the language of the posting. German applications often also include scanned certificates and references as attachments, bundled into one PDF.

## The Italian CV

Italy leans on the **[Europass CV](./europass-cv.md)** more than most countries. Many employers and nearly all public bodies recognise and expect it. Italian CVs commonly include a **photo** and personal data such as date of birth and nationality, which is normal across much of continental Europe.

One detail that surprises newcomers: Italian CVs conventionally end with a **GDPR consent line** authorising the employer to process your personal data (often referencing the Italian privacy code and EU Regulation 2016/679). Including it is standard and expected.

For private-sector and international roles in Italy — especially in tech, design, and multinationals — a clean English resume is often fine, and sometimes preferred. When in doubt for a public or academic application, Europass is the safe choice.

## The international English resume

For jobs at international companies, startups, and most English-speaking workplaces, the expectation is a **clean one-page resume** (early-career) led by achievements rather than duties. No photo, no date of birth, no personal data beyond contact details — partly to avoid bias, partly because hiring systems and norms in these markets are built around it.

This is also the format most likely to pass through software cleanly, which matters because large employers screen with applicant tracking systems. See [ATS-friendly resumes](./ats-friendly-resume.md) for the formatting rules that keep it parseable.

## CVs for university and scholarship applications

Applying to study is different from applying for a job, and the CV shifts with it.

- **Lead with education.** For a Master's or scholarship application, your degree, grades, thesis topic, and relevant coursework belong near the top, above work experience.
- **Show research and projects.** Academic admissions and scholarships care about research experience, publications, conference participation, relevant projects, and lab or fieldwork. List them with specifics.
- **Europass is often expected.** DAAD applications, Erasmus+, and Italian "right to study" scholarships frequently accept or prefer Europass because it standardises comparison. Check each program's instructions.
- **Pair it with a motivation letter.** Scholarship and admission committees read the motivation letter closely. Tailor it to the program and name concrete reasons you fit it.

## Which to use when

- **A job in Germany** → a German-style Lebenslauf plus an Anschreiben, in the language of the posting.
- **A job in Italy, public sector or academia** → Europass.
- **A job at an international company or startop, anywhere** → a clean English resume, ATS-checked.
- **A university or scholarship application** → an education-led CV, often Europass, plus a strong motivation letter.
