## The short version

As of 2024/2025, a student in Germany typically needs somewhere **around €950 to €1,500 per month**, depending heavily on the city. Munich and Frankfurt sit at the top of that range (mostly because of rent), while cities like Leipzig, Aachen, Dresden, and many smaller university towns sit at the bottom. Treat these as ballpark figures and verify against current local data, since prices, especially rent, have been rising. The official Study in Germany portal and your university's student services (Studierendenwerk) publish up-to-date local estimates.

This number matters beyond budgeting: it is closely tied to the **blocked account** amount you must show for a student visa. See [visa-and-blocked-account.md](visa-and-blocked-account.md), where the required figure is set roughly to a year's basic living costs.

## Rent

Rent is the single biggest expense and the one that varies most by city.

| City type | Examples | Rough monthly rent feel |
|---|---|---|
| Most expensive | Munich, Frankfurt, Stuttgart, Hamburg | High; a private room or small flat costs noticeably more |
| Mid-range | Cologne, Berlin (rising fast), Bonn, Düsseldorf | Moderate, but Berlin's market has tightened sharply |
| Cheaper | Leipzig, Dresden, Aachen, Göttingen, smaller towns | Lower; your money goes much further |

The cheapest housing is almost always a room in a **student dormitory** run by the local **Studierendenwerk** (the public student-services organization). Demand far exceeds supply, so apply as early as you possibly can, ideally as soon as you have an admission letter, because waiting lists are long. The next cheapest option is a **WG** (Wohngemeinschaft), a shared flat where you rent one room.

Be aware that the rental markets in **Munich and Berlin** are genuinely tight, and finding a room there can take real persistence and time. Budget for the possibility of temporary accommodation while you search, and start looking before you arrive.

## Health insurance

Health insurance is mandatory to enroll. Students under a certain age (and within a number of semesters) usually qualify for the reduced **public student health insurance** rate. As of 2024/2025 this is **around €120 per month** (the exact figure shifts with the statutory rates and includes long-term care insurance), so verify the current amount with a public insurer. Older students or those past the standard study duration may not qualify for the student rate and can pay more, so check your situation directly with a provider. Public student insurance is the standard and recommended route for most enrolled students.

## Food and groceries

Cooking at home keeps food costs reasonable. As a rough guide, many students spend somewhere in the region of a couple of hundred euros a month on groceries, less if you shop at the discount supermarkets (Aldi, Lidl, Netto) and cook for yourself, more if you eat out often. University canteens (**Mensa**) offer subsidized meals at low prices and are one of the best deals available to students. Verify against your own habits and city.

## Semester ticket and transport

At most universities, your **semester fee** includes a **semester ticket** that covers local (and sometimes regional) public transport, so day-to-day travel is effectively prepaid. The exact coverage and price differ by university and region. Separately, Germany has a nationwide discounted public-transport pass (the "Deutschlandticket"); some universities integrate a version of it into the semester ticket, so check what your university offers before buying anything extra.

## The broadcasting fee (Rundfunkbeitrag)

Every household in Germany pays a public **broadcasting fee** (Rundfunkbeitrag), regardless of whether you own a TV. It is charged per household, not per person, so flatmates in a shared flat split one fee. As of 2024/2025 it is **around €18–19 per month per household** — verify the current rate, since it is set periodically. If you live in a WG, only one person registers and pays, and you divide it. Certain low-income students (for example, some BAföG recipients) can apply for an exemption.

## Phone, internet, and the rest

Budget a modest amount for a mobile plan and, if it is not included in your rent, home internet. Prepaid SIM plans are cheap and easy to start with. Leave room in your budget for books and materials, clothing, leisure, and the occasional unexpected cost.

## Putting it together

A realistic monthly picture, as of 2024/2025 and varying by city, looks roughly like this. Verify the figures locally.

| Category | Rough monthly range |
|---|---|
| Rent (room in dorm or WG) | The largest item; far higher in Munich/Frankfurt than in Leipzig/Aachen |
| Health insurance (public student rate) | Around €120 |
| Food / groceries | A couple of hundred euros |
| Semester ticket | Often included in the semester fee |
| Broadcasting fee | Around €18–19 per household (split in a WG) |
| Phone, internet, leisure, misc. | A modest additional sum |
| **Typical total** | **Around €950–€1,500/month, city-dependent** |

The bottom of that range is realistic in a cheaper city if you live in a dorm and cook for yourself; the top reflects an expensive city with a private room. Keep some buffer beyond the minimum, because the first month (deposit, setup costs) is always the most expensive.
