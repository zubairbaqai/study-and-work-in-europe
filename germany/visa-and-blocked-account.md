## Do you even need a visa?

If you are an **EU/EEA or Swiss** citizen, you do not need a visa or a residence permit to study in Germany; you just register locally after you arrive. **Non-EU** citizens usually need a visa, with a few exceptions: nationals of a handful of countries (such as the US, Canada, Australia, Japan, and a few others) can enter without a visa and apply for the residence permit after arrival. Everyone else applies for a national visa at the German embassy or consulate before traveling. Check your specific case on the German Federal Foreign Office site at https://www.auswaertiges-amt.de.

## The national (D) student visa

For studying a full degree, you apply for a **national visa (D visa)** for study purposes, not a short-stay Schengen visa. There is also a separate "student applicant" visa if you still need to apply to universities or sort out admission steps once in Germany, which later converts to a study visa.

You apply at the German mission in your country, and the typical documents include:

- Your admission letter (Zulassungsbescheid), or proof you are in the application process for the applicant visa.
- Proof of financial means for the first year (most commonly a blocked account — see below).
- Proof of health insurance.
- Your passport, application form, biometric photos, and academic documents (including an APS certificate if your country requires one — see [admissions.md](admissions.md)).
- Possibly proof of language ability matching your program.

Appointment waiting times at German missions can be long in some countries, so book your visa appointment as early as you can once you have your admission. Requirements vary slightly by country, so the embassy's own checklist is the authority.

## The blocked account (Sperrkonto)

The most common way to prove you can support yourself is a **blocked account** (Sperrkonto). You deposit a year's worth of basic living costs into a special account before you arrive, and once in Germany you can withdraw only a set maximum amount each month. This guarantees you have funds without being able to spend it all at once.

As of 2024, the required amount was **around €11,904 for the year, which works out to roughly €992 per month**. This figure is reviewed and updated periodically (it tends to rise), so you must verify the **current** required amount before you open the account. Confirm it on the German Federal Foreign Office site (https://www.auswaertiges-amt.de) and on your destination embassy's page.

Common providers that international students use to open a blocked account are **Fintiba** and **Expatrio**, among others. They are mentioned here neutrally as examples; compare their fees and terms yourself, and note that German banks and some others also offer blocked accounts. There is no single official provider.

Note that a blocked account is the usual route but not the only accepted proof of funds; a formal sponsorship declaration (Verpflichtungserklärung) from someone in Germany, or certain scholarships, can also satisfy the financial requirement. Check what your embassy accepts. The living-cost side of this number is covered in [living-costs.md](living-costs.md).

## Health insurance

You must have valid health insurance to get the visa and to enroll. For the visa application and your first days, travel/incoming insurance may be needed, but once you enroll, most students take **public student health insurance** (the standard route for enrolled students), at a reduced student rate. See the figure and details in [living-costs.md](living-costs.md). Sort this out before your visa appointment, because proof of insurance is part of the visa file.

## After you arrive: the residence permit

The visa gets you into Germany; it is valid for a limited initial period. After you arrive, you register your address (Anmeldung) at the local registration office, then apply at the local immigration office (Ausländerbehörde) for a **residence permit for study purposes** (an **Aufenthaltstitel** / Aufenthaltserlaubnis zu Studienzwecken). This is the document that lets you stay for the duration of your studies, and you renew it as needed. Bring your enrollment certificate, proof of funds, proof of insurance, your passport, and registration confirmation. Book this appointment early too, as immigration offices in big cities get busy.

## After you graduate: staying to work

This is the part that makes Germany attractive for international students. After you finish your degree, you can apply to extend your residence permit to **look for a job** related to your qualification. As of recent rules this job-seeking permit runs for up to **18 months** after graduation, during which you may take work to support yourself while searching. Verify the current duration and conditions, since immigration rules change.

Once you have a qualified job offer, you switch to a work residence permit. For well-paid, qualified roles, the **EU Blue Card** is the main route; it has a minimum salary threshold that changes yearly and offers a faster path toward permanent residence. The details, the current salary thresholds, and the in-demand fields are covered in [working-and-jobs.md](working-and-jobs.md). For the official, current rules on post-study work and the Blue Card, use the German government's portal for international skilled workers at https://www.make-it-in-germany.com.
