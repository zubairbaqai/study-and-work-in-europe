## How German universities work

Most German universities are public, and tuition is free or close to free for everyone, including international students, in most federal states. You usually pay only a "semester fee" (Semesterbeitrag) of a few hundred euros, which often includes a public transport ticket. A few states charge tuition for non-EU students (Baden-Württemberg is the well-known case, with a fee for non-EU students at public universities), so check the state your university is in.

There are two main kinds of institution. Universities (Universität, TU for technical ones) are research-focused and can award doctorates. Universities of applied sciences (Hochschule für angewandte Wissenschaften, often "HAW" or "FH") are more practical and industry-linked. This guide focuses on the research universities, since those are what most people mean by "top German universities."

## The universities

**Technical University of Munich (TUM)** — Consistently rated Germany's strongest technical university. Famous for engineering, computer science, physics, and increasingly for entrepreneurship and life sciences. Strong industry ties around Munich.

**Ludwig Maximilian University of Munich (LMU)** — A large, classic research university. Known for medicine, physics, law, the humanities, and the natural sciences. Frequently sits at the top of national rankings alongside TUM.

**Heidelberg University** — Germany's oldest university (founded 1386) and a research heavyweight. Famous for medicine and the life sciences, physics, law, and the humanities, with a major biomedical cluster around it.

**RWTH Aachen** — The go-to name for engineering in Germany. Mechanical engineering, electrical engineering, materials science, and automotive/production engineering are its signature. Deeply tied to German industry.

**Technical University of Berlin (TU Berlin)** — Strong across engineering, computer science, and planning, in a city with a large tech and startup scene. Good for those who want a technical degree plus a big-city ecosystem.

**Humboldt University of Berlin** — A historic research university known for the humanities, social sciences, law, and the natural sciences. Pairs naturally with the cultural and academic density of Berlin.

**Karlsruhe Institute of Technology (KIT)** — A merger of a university and a national research center. Excellent for computer science, engineering, physics, and energy research. A serious choice for CS and informatics.

**University of Stuttgart** — An engineering powerhouse in the heart of Germany's automotive region (Porsche, Mercedes, Bosch nearby). Strong in mechanical and aerospace engineering, automotive, and manufacturing.

**University of Freiburg** — A well-rounded research university in the south-west. Known for medicine, the life sciences, environmental and forestry sciences, and the humanities.

**University of Göttingen** — A traditional research university with deep history in the natural sciences and mathematics. Strong in physics, chemistry, and the sciences generally, with many Nobel laureates in its past.

**University of Mannheim** — The standout for business, economics, and the social sciences in Germany, with a reputation and structure closer to a US-style business school. If you want a German degree in management, economics, or political/social science, this is the first name to look at.

**Goethe University Frankfurt** — Located in Germany's financial capital. Strong in economics, finance, law, and the social sciences, with obvious advantages for anyone aiming at the banking and finance sector.

**Technical University of Darmstadt (TU Darmstadt)** — A respected technical university near Frankfurt. Known for computer science, electrical and mechanical engineering, and IT security in particular.

**University of Bonn** — A broad research university with particular strength in mathematics, economics, and the natural sciences. Its mathematics and economics departments are among the best in the country.

**Technical University of Dresden (TU Dresden)** — One of the larger technical universities, strong in engineering, microelectronics, and the sciences, and a good-value option in eastern Germany with lower living costs.

## How to apply

The application path depends on the university and the program, so the first rule is to read the specific program page on the university's website. That said, the system has a clear shape.

### uni-assist vs. applying directly

Many German universities outsource the checking of international applications to a central service called **uni-assist** (the "Arbeits- und Servicestelle für internationale Studienbewerbungen"). uni-assist receives your documents, verifies that your foreign certificates meet the formal requirements, converts your grades to the German scale, and forwards qualifying applications to the university. You apply through the uni-assist portal at https://www.uni-assist.de and pay a handling fee per application (the first application in a semester costs more than each additional one — check the current fees on their site).

Other universities run their own international office and take applications directly through their own online portal, with no uni-assist step. Some use uni-assist only for certain programs. There is no universal rule, so on each program's admissions page look for whether it says "apply via uni-assist" or "apply directly to the university." Applying the wrong way is a common reason applications get delayed or rejected.

### Winter vs. summer intake and deadlines

Germany has two intakes. The **winter semester** starts around October and is the main intake, with the widest choice of programs. The **summer semester** starts around April and has fewer programs open, especially for first-year bachelor's students.

Deadlines vary by university and program, but as a rough guide, applications for the winter intake are **typically** due around **15 July**, and applications for the summer intake around **mid-January**. Treat these as orientation only. Many master's programs, and most applications routed through uni-assist, have earlier deadlines (uni-assist needs time to process before forwarding). Always confirm the exact date on the specific program page.

### Document requirements

The core question is whether your school-leaving certificate is recognized as equivalent to the German Abitur for direct university entry. You can check this in the DAAD admission database and in the official **anabin** database (see [admissions.md](admissions.md) for the step-by-step).

If your certificate is **not** directly recognized, the usual path is a **Studienkolleg**: a preparatory year that ends with an assessment exam called the **Feststellungsprüfung**. Passing it qualifies you for university entry in the relevant subject stream. This applies to many applicants whose secondary education is shorter or structured differently from the German system.

Students from **China, India, and Vietnam** generally need an **APS certificate** (Akademische Prüfstelle), which verifies your academic documents before you apply. You obtain it through the APS portal for your country, and you should start this early because it takes time. Build it into your timeline well before the application deadline.

Beyond that, expect to provide your secondary certificate and transcripts (and your bachelor's degree and transcripts for a master's), proof of language ability, a CV, your passport, and program-specific items such as a statement of purpose, letters of recommendation, or a portfolio.

### Language requirements

For **German-taught programs**, you typically need to prove German at roughly C1 level through either the **TestDaF** or the **DSH** exam (the DSH is taken at a German university; TestDaF can be taken in many countries). Some programs accept other certificates such as the Goethe-Zertifikat or telc.

For **English-taught master's programs**, you usually need **IELTS** or **TOEFL**, with the minimum score set by the program. Many English-taught programs do not require German for admission, though you will still want some German for daily life and for working afterward.
