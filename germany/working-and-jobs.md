## Working while you study

Non-EU students in Germany are allowed to work a limited amount alongside their studies without a separate work permit. As of 2024 the limit was **raised**, and students may work **140 full days or 280 half days per year**. Because this rule was recently changed, confirm the **current** allowance and the exact definitions before relying on it, on the official Make it in Germany portal (https://www.make-it-in-germany.com) and with your local immigration office.

A few practical notes. Work during the semester is usually best kept part-time so it does not hurt your studies, and your residence permit's conditions still apply. Working **more** than the allowance generally requires approval from the immigration office and the employment agency. Working-student roles (see below) can have their own treatment of these limits, so ask the employer and the immigration office how a specific job counts.

## Minijobs

A **Minijob** is a low-hours, low-pay job with a monthly earnings ceiling, taxed and treated in a simplified way. The earnings limit is tied to the minimum wage and is adjusted over time, so check the current monthly ceiling rather than assuming a fixed figure. Minijobs are common for students because they are easy to take on (cafés, retail, campus jobs), but they pay relatively little and rarely build career-relevant experience. They are a fine way to top up your budget, especially early on.

## Werkstudent: the working-student path

The strongest move during your studies is a **Werkstudent** (working-student) position. These are part-time roles, usually around 20 hours per week during the semester, at real companies and in your field of study. They pay considerably better than a Minijob, count as proper professional experience, and very often lead to a full-time offer at the same company after graduation.

For international students aiming to stay and work in Germany, lining up a Werkstudent job in your field is the single most effective thing you can do, because it gives you German work experience, a professional network, and a foot in the door before you even graduate. Engineering, IT, finance, and many science fields have plenty of Werkstudent openings at larger employers. Note that working-student hours interact with the student work allowance, so confirm the rules with your employer and the immigration office.

## After graduation: the 18-month job-seeker permit

When you finish your degree, you can apply to extend your stay to look for a qualified job. As of recent rules this **job-seeker residence permit** lasts up to **18 months** after graduation, and during it you are allowed to work to support yourself while you search. Verify the current duration and conditions, since immigration rules are updated periodically. This window is generous by international standards and is one of the main reasons Germany is attractive for international graduates. See [visa-and-blocked-account.md](visa-and-blocked-account.md) for how this fits into the residence-permit sequence.

## The EU Blue Card and salary thresholds

Once you have a qualified job offer that meets a minimum salary, the **EU Blue Card** is the main work-and-residence route for university graduates. It offers a faster track to permanent residence and easier conditions for family members.

There is a **minimum gross annual salary threshold** to qualify, with a **lower threshold for shortage occupations and for recent graduates** in certain fields. These thresholds are **set and adjusted every year**, so any specific number here would go stale quickly. Look up the current figures on the official portal before you plan around them.

- Make it in Germany (EU Blue Card, current salary thresholds): https://www.make-it-in-germany.com

## Fields with strong demand

Germany has well-documented skill shortages in several areas, which is good news if your qualification is in one of them:

- **Engineering** (mechanical, electrical, automotive, civil).
- **IT and software** (developers, data, IT security).
- **Healthcare** (doctors, nurses, care professionals).
- **Skilled trades** and technical professions.
- **Natural sciences and mathematics** in many roles.

Demand shifts over time and by region, so check the current official shortage-occupation information on Make it in Germany. If your field is on the shortage list, both job-hunting and the Blue Card route tend to be easier.

## Preparing your application

German employers expect a particular application style, and getting it right matters.

The German CV is called a **Lebenslauf**. It is concise and tabular, organized in clear sections (personal details, work experience, education, skills, languages), usually **one to two pages**, and written in reverse-chronological order. A **photo** at the top is a long-standing German convention, though it is becoming optional and many employers no longer expect it, so use your judgment. Keep it factual and free of long prose; the detail belongs in the cover letter.

Alongside the CV, a strong **motivation letter** (Anschreiben / cover letter) is expected for most applications. It should be tailored to the specific role and company, explain why you fit, and be well written in the language of the job posting (German for German-language roles, English for English-language ones). Many German applications also include a set of certificates and references as attachments.
