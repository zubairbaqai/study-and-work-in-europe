## Step 1: Check the program and its language

Start on the program's own page. Confirm the degree level, the language of instruction (German-taught or English-taught), the exact entry requirements, and how they want you to apply. Two programs at the same university can have different rules, so never assume. If the program is German-taught you will need German proficiency (usually around C1 via TestDaF or DSH); if it is English-taught you will usually need IELTS or TOEFL. See [universities.md](universities.md) for the language detail.

## Step 2: Check whether your certificate is recognized

The central question for bachelor's applicants is whether your school-leaving certificate counts as equivalent to the German Abitur, which gives you a "higher education entrance qualification" (Hochschulzugangsberechtigung).

Two official tools tell you:

- The **DAAD admission database**, which lets you enter your country and certificate and tells you what it qualifies you for.
- **anabin**, the official database run by the Conference of Education Ministers, at https://anabin.kmk.org. It is in German, but it is the authoritative reference that university offices themselves use.

There are three possible outcomes. Your certificate may give **direct access** to a German university. It may give access only **after a Studienkolleg** (see the next step). Or it may give access to certain subjects only, or require a year or two of university study in your home country first. Check this before you do anything else, because it determines your whole timeline.

## Step 3: The Studienkolleg path

If your certificate is not recognized for direct entry, the standard route is a **Studienkolleg**, a one-year preparatory college that brings you up to the German university entry standard. It is organized into subject-specific streams (for example, a technical stream for engineering, an economics stream, a medical/science stream).

The Studienkolleg ends with an assessment exam, the **Feststellungsprüfung**. Passing it gives you the entrance qualification for university study in that subject stream. Note that most Studienkollegs teach and examine in German, so you usually need German at around B1–B2 to be admitted to the Studienkolleg itself, and stronger German by the end. There are public Studienkollegs (low or no cost) and private ones (fee-based). Plan for this extra year if your certificate requires it.

## Step 4: Understand numerus clausus (NC)

Some programs are open admission: if you meet the requirements, you get in. Others are **restricted** because they have more applicants than places, and these are called **numerus clausus (NC)** programs. For an NC program, places are allocated mainly by grade (your converted German grade average), sometimes combined with other criteria or waiting time.

A key point that confuses people: the "NC" is not a fixed cutoff published in advance. It is simply the grade of the last applicant who got a place in a given semester, so it moves up and down each year depending on who applied. Medicine and psychology are the classic high-NC subjects. If a program is NC-restricted, a stronger grade average improves your chances, and you should apply to several programs to spread your risk.

## Step 5: The APS certificate

If you studied in **China, India, or Vietnam**, you generally need an **APS certificate** from the Akademische Prüfstelle before you can apply. The APS verifies that your academic documents are genuine and that your qualifications are what you say they are. You apply through the APS portal for your country, and the process includes a document check and, in some cases, an interview.

This takes time, sometimes several weeks or more, so begin it well before any application deadline. Many universities will not process your application, and German missions will not issue a student visa, without a valid APS certificate where one is required.

## Step 6: Apply through uni-assist or directly

How you submit depends on the university:

- **Via uni-assist** (https://www.uni-assist.de): you create an account, upload your documents, select your programs, and pay a handling fee per application. uni-assist checks your documents formally, converts your grades to the German scale, and forwards qualifying applications to the universities. The first application in a semester costs more, with a lower fee for each additional one — check current fees on their site.
- **Directly to the university's international office**: you use the university's own portal, with no uni-assist step.

Some universities use uni-assist for some programs and direct application for others. The program page always says which. Submitting through the wrong channel is a common, avoidable mistake.

## Step 7: Deadlines

Germany has a winter intake (starts around October, the main intake) and a smaller summer intake (starts around April). As a rough orientation, winter-intake applications are **typically** due around **15 July** and summer-intake applications around **mid-January**, but deadlines vary widely by university and program, and uni-assist deadlines are often earlier because processing takes time. Confirm the exact date on the specific program page and work backward from it, especially if you also need an APS certificate or a Studienkolleg place.

## Step 8: Your admission decision

If you are accepted, you receive an admission letter, the **Zulassungsbescheid**. It can be one of two kinds:

- **Unconditional admission**: you have met all requirements and can enroll.
- **Conditional admission**: you are admitted provided you fulfil specific conditions before enrollment, such as reaching a required language level, submitting a final transcript, or completing a missing document. Read the conditions carefully and meet them by the stated date.

The admission letter is also the document you use to apply for your student visa and to enroll (Immatrikulation) once you arrive. See [visa-and-blocked-account.md](visa-and-blocked-account.md) for the visa steps that follow.
