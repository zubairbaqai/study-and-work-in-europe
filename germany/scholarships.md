## How funding usually works in Germany

A useful thing to understand early: in Germany, a scholarship is often not the whole answer, because tuition is already free or very low at most public universities. Many international students fund their studies through a **combination** of free tuition, a part-time or working-student job, and a scholarship or stipend on top. So even a partial scholarship can go a long way, and you should think about all three sources together rather than waiting for one large award. For the work side, see [working-and-jobs.md](working-and-jobs.md).

## DAAD scholarships

The **DAAD** (German Academic Exchange Service) runs the largest set of scholarships for international students and researchers, and it is the first place to look. It funds master's students, doctoral candidates, researchers, and short programs, with offerings that vary by your country and field. Some are full scholarships covering a monthly stipend plus allowances; others are partial or for specific durations.

Use the DAAD scholarship database to filter by your status and country, since eligibility and amounts differ a lot between programs. Stipend levels and what each scholarship covers change over time, so read the exact figures on the specific scholarship page rather than relying on a single quoted number.

- DAAD scholarship database: https://www.daad.de

## Deutschlandstipendium

The **Deutschlandstipendium** is a merit-based scholarship awarded through individual universities, open to students of any nationality enrolled at a participating German university. It is half-funded by private sponsors and half by the federal government.

As of 2024/2025 it is **around €300 per month**, and it is awarded for at least two semesters and can be extended — but verify the current amount and conditions, because they can change. It is not income-dependent and is based on achievement and potential. You apply at your own university, not centrally, so check your university's website for its application window.

- Official information: https://www.deutschlandstipendium.de

## Erasmus+

**Erasmus+** is the EU program mainly used for **exchange** rather than a full degree. If you are already enrolled at a university (in Europe or at a partner institution) and want to spend a semester or two in Germany as part of your home degree, Erasmus+ can fund the mobility period with a grant. It is less relevant if you want to do an entire degree in Germany from scratch, and more relevant for structured exchanges between partner universities. Apply through your home university's international office.

- Official information: https://erasmus-plus.ec.europa.eu

## The political and church foundations (Begabtenförderungswerke)

Germany has a set of foundations, partly publicly funded, that award scholarships to talented students, including international ones, often combining financial support with a network and seminar program. These are collectively known as the **Begabtenförderungswerke**. They each have their own political or value orientation, and they expect applicants to share an interest in their aims, alongside strong academic performance and social engagement.

The main ones include:

- **Heinrich Böll Foundation** — close to green/ecological and human-rights values. https://www.boell.de
- **Konrad-Adenauer-Stiftung (KAS)** — close to Christian-democratic values. https://www.kas.de
- **Friedrich-Ebert-Stiftung (FES)** — close to social-democratic values. https://www.fes.de

Other foundations in this group include the Friedrich-Naumann-Stiftung (liberal), the Hanns-Seidel-Stiftung, the Rosa-Luxemburg-Stiftung (left), and the church-affiliated Cusanuswerk (Catholic) and Evangelisches Studienwerk Villigst (Protestant). Several of these fund international students, but eligibility rules vary, so check each foundation's own page.

What they cover and the stipend amounts vary by foundation and by your situation (for example, master's vs. doctoral), so treat any single figure with caution and confirm on the foundation's website. Many of these stipends are structured similarly to the public student-funding rates, but the details and eligibility for non-EU students differ between foundations.

## A note on BAföG

**BAföG** is Germany's state student-funding system. It is generally aimed at German nationals and certain EU citizens, and at some international students who have lived and worked in Germany for a longer period or have particular residence statuses. As a newly arriving non-EU student, you usually will **not** qualify for BAföG, so do not count on it in your funding plan. If you think your residence history might make you eligible, check the official BAföG information and your local student services office.

- Official BAföG information: https://www.bafög.de
