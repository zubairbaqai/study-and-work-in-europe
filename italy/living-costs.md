# Living Costs in Italy

A realistic look at what it costs to live as a student in Italy each month, broken down by category and by city, plus how health coverage and tuition work. Costs vary a lot between the expensive north and cheaper southern cities, so treat every number here as a range to verify, not a quote.

## The short version

For a student, a realistic total of roughly **€700–€1,200 per month** covers most cases as of 2024/2025, but where you land in that range depends heavily on the city. Milan, Rome, and Bologna sit at the top; many southern cities sit well below it. This is a planning estimate, not a fixed figure, so verify current rents and prices for your specific city before you budget. The biggest single variable is rent, especially whether you get a place in subsidised student housing or rent privately.

## Costs by category

These are typical monthly ranges for a single student. Actual figures move with the city and with your own habits, so check local listings.

| Category | Rough monthly range (verify locally) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Rent (room in shared flat) | the largest item; far higher in Milan than in the south | A private studio costs much more; student housing through the regional DSU body is the cheapest option if you qualify. |
| Food and groceries | moderate | Cheaper if you cook; university canteens (mensa) are heavily subsidised, especially with a DSU place. |
| Public transport | low | Most cities offer discounted student season tickets. |
| Phone and internet | low | Italian mobile plans are inexpensive. |
| Books, supplies, personal | low to moderate | Varies by program. |
| Health coverage | see below | An annual lump sum rather than a monthly cost. |

Because rent dominates the total, your city choice and housing situation decide your budget more than anything else.

## Costs by city

A rough guide to relative cost of living, not exact numbers:

- **Most expensive:** Milan, then Rome, Bologna, and other large northern student cities. Milan in particular has high rents.
- **Mid-range:** Turin, Padua, Florence, Pisa, Trento, Trieste.
- **Cheaper:** Naples and many southern cities, where rent and everyday costs are noticeably lower.

If budget is your main concern, a southern city or a smaller university town stretches the same money much further than Milan does. Always confirm with current local rental listings, since rents have moved a lot in recent years.

## Health coverage

Non-EU students arrange health cover in one of two ways:

- **Voluntary registration with the SSN** (Servizio Sanitario Nazionale, the national health service). This gives you access to a GP and the public system on the same basis as residents. Registration runs by **calendar year** (1 January to 31 December) and is paid as an annual lump sum. As of 2024/2025, the annual fee for voluntary SSN registration is **around €700 per year** (set by Law 213/2023, the 2024 budget law). Verify the current amount before you pay, since budget laws change it. ([Law 213/2023 overview, Mazzeschi](https://www.mazzeschi.it/annual-fee-for-non-eu-nationals-adhering-to-the-italian-national-health-service/); confirm via your local ASL.)
- **Private health insurance**, a policy that meets the visa requirement. This is often used for the visa and the first weeks before you can register with the SSN.

Many students take private insurance to satisfy the visa, then switch to voluntary SSN registration after arrival because it is usually better value and gives full access to the public system. Check your university's welcome office, which walks students through the local process.

## Tuition is income-based

Public university tuition in Italy is set largely by your **ISEE** (or **ISEE Parificato** for families abroad), so it scales with your household's economic situation. The practical effect is that public universities are far cheaper than in many countries, and for low-income students a fee waiver through a regional DSU scholarship can bring tuition close to nothing.

This is one of the strongest reasons to sort out your ISEE Parificato early. For how the waivers and scholarships work, see [scholarships.md](./scholarships.md). For the exact tuition you would pay, check the tuition/fees page of your specific university, since each one publishes its own income-based fee bands.
