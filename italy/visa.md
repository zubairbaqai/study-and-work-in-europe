# Student Visa and Residence Permit

What a non-EU student needs to legally study and live in Italy: the type D student visa, the residence permit you apply for after arrival, and what happens to your status after you graduate. Visa rules change, so confirm every requirement against the official sources before you rely on it.

## Before the visa: pre-enrollment

The visa is not the first step. You first complete **pre-enrollment on Universitaly** ([universitaly.it](https://www.universitaly.it)) through the Italian embassy or consulate that covers your area. Only after the consulate has validated your pre-enrollment do you apply for the student visa at that same consulate. The two steps are joined, and the consulate is your point of contact for both.

## The type D student visa

Non-EU students apply for a **national long-stay visa, type D**, for study. You apply at the Italian embassy or consulate responsible for your country or region. This visa lets you enter Italy to take up your study place; it is the residence permit you apply for after arrival that lets you stay for the longer term.

The exact document list, appointment process, and any fee are set by your consulate, so follow that consulate's instructions. The official entry point is [studyinitaly.esteri.it](https://studyinitaly.esteri.it), and visa specifics are on the foreign ministry's visa portal at [vistoperitalia.esteri.it](https://vistoperitalia.esteri.it).

## What you provide for the visa

The standard requirements for a study visa generally include:

- **Validated pre-enrollment / admission** to the Italian program.
- **Proof of sufficient funds** to support yourself for the study period. The consulate sets the required amount and how you prove it (bank statements, sponsorship, scholarship award).
- **Proof of accommodation** in Italy.
- **Health insurance** valid in Italy for the visa period. After arrival many students switch to voluntary SSN registration (see [living-costs.md](./living-costs.md)).
- A valid passport, photos, and the consulate's application forms.

Amounts and document specifics vary by consulate and change over time, so take the figures and the list from your consulate and from [vistoperitalia.esteri.it](https://vistoperitalia.esteri.it), not from memory.

## After you arrive: the residence permit

The visa gets you into Italy. To stay, you apply for a **permesso di soggiorno** (residence permit) for study.

1. **Apply within 8 working days of arrival.** You are expected to start the residence-permit application within eight working days of entering Italy.
2. **Get the kit at the post office.** Pick up the residence-permit application kit (the "kit giallo") at a **Poste Italiane** post office that handles them (Sportello Amico), fill it in, and submit it there. You pay the relevant fees and stamp duty at this stage.
3. **Questura appointment.** The post office gives you an appointment at the **questura** (police headquarters) for fingerprinting and document checks.
4. **Collect the permit.** After processing, you collect the residence-permit card. Keep the receipt from the post office in the meantime, since it proves your application is pending.

Your university's international or welcome office usually guides students through this, because the local process and the required documents differ slightly by city.

## Codice fiscale

The **codice fiscale** is the Italian tax identification code, and you will need it for almost everything: enrollment, a rental contract, a bank account, an SSN registration, a phone plan. You can obtain it from the **Agenzia delle Entrate** (the tax agency) or, in some cases, through the consulate or your university. Get it early, because other steps depend on it.

## After you graduate

There are two main paths after your degree:

- **Convert your study permit to a work permit.** If you find qualifying employment or self-employment, your study residence permit can be converted to a work permit. This conversion generally has to fit within Italy's annual immigration quota system (the **Decreto Flussi**), so timing and quota availability matter. See [working-and-jobs.md](./working-and-jobs.md) for how this works.
- **Apply for a post-study residence permit to look for work or continue studying.** Italy provides a residence permit, commonly cited as up to **12 months**, that lets a recent graduate stay to look for a job or pursue further study after finishing their degree. The exact duration, eligibility, and conditions change, so verify the current rules before relying on them.

Both routes change with immigration law and quota decrees. Confirm the current rules on [studyinitaly.esteri.it](https://studyinitaly.esteri.it) and [vistoperitalia.esteri.it](https://vistoperitalia.esteri.it), and ask your university's office, which tends to know the latest local practice.
