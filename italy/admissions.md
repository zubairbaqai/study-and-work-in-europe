# Admissions: The Step-by-Step Process

How a non-EU student actually gets from picking a program to holding an enrollment place at an Italian university. This walks through the full procedure in order.

## The process in order

For most non-EU applicants the sequence looks like this:

1. Choose a program and confirm its admission rules.
2. Apply for admission to the university and sit any required entrance test.
3. Pre-enroll on Universitaly through the Italian embassy or consulate.
4. Get your foreign qualification recognised (Dichiarazione di Valore or a CIMEA statement).
5. Prove your language level.
6. Receive your place against the non-EU quota and proceed to the visa.

The university channel and the government channel run in parallel, which trips people up. You are dealing with the university's own application portal and with Universitaly plus the consulate at the same time.

## Step 1: Choose your program

Pick the program first, then read its admission page carefully. Each university sets its own requirements, entrance tests, and deadlines on top of the national rules. The national starting point for international applicants is [studyinitaly.esteri.it](https://studyinitaly.esteri.it), which explains the overall procedure and links to the universities.

Confirm three things before you go further: whether the program is taught in Italian or English, what entrance test (if any) it requires, and whether it has a fixed number of places.

## Step 2: Pre-enrollment on Universitaly

Non-EU students applying from abroad must complete a **pre-enrollment** application on [universitaly.it](https://www.universitaly.it). You select your program and the Italian diplomatic mission (embassy or consulate) that covers where you live. The mission then validates your pre-enrollment, and this is the step that connects your study place to your visa application.

Pre-enrollment is not the same as being admitted. It is the government-side registration of your intention to study, which the consulate needs before it can issue a student visa.

## Step 3: Get your qualification recognised

Italy needs to compare your foreign diploma or degree to its own system. There are two common routes:

- **Dichiarazione di Valore (Declaration of Value)** — issued by the Italian embassy or consulate, describing your qualification and its standing in your home country.
- **CIMEA Statement of Comparability** — issued by CIMEA, the Italian ENIC-NARIC centre, at [cimea.it](https://www.cimea.it). Many universities now accept this instead of the Dichiarazione di Valore, and it is often faster to obtain.

Ask your university's admissions office which one they require before you start, because requesting the wrong document wastes weeks.

## Step 4: Entrance requirements and tests

Which test applies depends on the program:

- **IMAT** for English-taught Medicine and Surgery degrees.
- **TOLC** tests, run by **CISIA** ([cisia.it](https://www.cisia.it)), for many bachelor's programs in engineering, economics, and the sciences. The specific TOLC (for example TOLC-I or TOLC-E) depends on the field.
- **Program-specific exams** set by individual universities, common for competitive master's programs.

Some programs admit on documents alone (transcripts, motivation, references) with no test. The program page is the only reliable source for which path applies to you.

## Step 5: Language certification

- **Italian-taught programs** generally expect around B2 Italian, often proven with a CILS, CELI, or PLIDA certificate. Some universities run their own language assessment.
- **English-taught programs** generally expect IELTS or TOEFL at a level the program sets, with some programs accepting equivalents or granting waivers.

The required level and accepted certificates vary by university, so check the program's page rather than assuming a national standard.

## Step 6: Non-EU quota places and the consulate

Italian universities reserve a set number of places for non-EU students who are resident abroad. You apply against this quota, and the consulate is the office that validates your pre-enrollment and later handles your student visa. Treat the embassy or consulate that covers your region as your main contact through this whole stage, since they sit between Universitaly and your visa.

## Deadlines

Deadlines are set per university and per program, and the national pre-enrollment window on Universitaly **typically opens in summer** ahead of the autumn academic year, but the exact dates change each year. Do not rely on a remembered date. Check the current year's calendar on [universitaly.it](https://www.universitaly.it) and on [studyinitaly.esteri.it](https://studyinitaly.esteri.it), and check your specific program's admission page, because university application deadlines often fall earlier than the pre-enrollment window.

Start early. Qualification recognition, language certificates, and consulate appointments each take time, and they have to line up before the visa stage.
