## Working while you study

Non-EU students on a study residence permit are allowed to work part-time. The limit is commonly cited as **up to about 20 hours per week, with an annual cap of around 1,040 hours**. If you concentrate your hours, for example during holidays, you can exceed 20 hours in a given week as long as you stay under the annual total. These figures come up consistently in guidance, but the exact rules and any updates should be checked against the official sources below before you rely on them. ([Work rules summary, Giambrone Law](https://www.giambronelaw.com/site/news-articles-press/library/articles/study-visa-for-italy-requirements-for-international-students); confirm via [studyinitaly.esteri.it](https://studyinitaly.esteri.it).)

If you need to work more than the study permit allows, that is the point where you have to look at a proper work permit instead.

## From study permit to work permit

After graduation, the main route to staying and working is converting your study residence permit into a work permit.

- **Conversion within the Decreto Flussi quota.** Italy sets an annual quota for work permits through a decree called the **Decreto Flussi**, and conversions of study permits to work permits generally have to fit within it. Because the quota opens in defined windows and can fill up, timing matters. Watch the decree's schedule and apply when conversions open.
- **Via the post-study permit.** Alternatively, the post-study residence permit (commonly cited as up to 12 months to seek work or continue studying, see [visa.md](./visa.md)) gives you a window to find a qualifying job before converting your status.

The specifics of conversion, the quota size, and the application windows change every year with the decree, so confirm the current process before counting on it.

## The EU Blue Card route

For qualified, higher-skilled roles, the **EU Blue Card** is a separate route that does not depend on the standard work quota in the same way. It is aimed at graduates and professionals with a recognised qualification and a job offer above a salary threshold. The qualification, salary threshold, and conditions are set by current rules, so check the official sources for the figures rather than assuming them. For a recent graduate from an Italian university with a skilled job offer, this can be a cleaner path than the quota conversion.

## Sectors that hire

Demand is uneven across fields and regions. Sectors that more often have openings include:

- **Engineering** (mechanical, electrical, civil), especially in the industrial north.
- **IT and software**, where English-speaking roles at larger and international companies are more common.
- **Design**, building on Italy's strength in product, fashion, and industrial design, much of it around Milan.
- **Tourism and hospitality**, seasonal and year-round, across the country.
- **Manufacturing**, concentrated in northern Italy (Lombardy, Piedmont, Emilia-Romagna, the Veneto).

The north (Milan, Turin, Bologna, the Veneto) has the densest job market for graduates, which is worth weighing when you choose where to study.

## How much Italian you need

Be honest with yourself about language. Outside multinationals, English-speaking tech roles, and academia, most Italian workplaces operate in Italian, and your job prospects improve sharply with solid Italian. Plenty of international graduates land roles at multinationals or English-working companies with limited Italian, but the wider market opens up once you can work in the language. If you plan to stay and work in Italy long term, treat learning Italian as part of the plan rather than an optional extra.

## Preparing your application

Italian and broader EU hiring conventions differ from those in the US or UK, so a quick orientation helps.

- **The Europass CV is widely accepted and often expected** in Italy and across the EU. It is a standard format many Italian employers and public bodies recognise immediately.
- **CVs in Italy may include a photo and personal data** such as date of birth and nationality, which is conventional in much of continental Europe even though it is avoided elsewhere.
- **A GDPR consent line is conventional** at the bottom of an Italian CV, authorising the employer to process your personal data. Including it is normal and expected.

For the detailed guides, see [Europass CV](../job-hunt/europass-cv.md), [ATS-friendly resumes](../job-hunt/ats-friendly-resume.md), and [German & Italian CV norms](../job-hunt/application-cv-by-country.md). If you want to build one online, tools like [Prezumi](https://www.prezumi.com) can help, but the format guides above matter more than any single tool.
