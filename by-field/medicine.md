# Studying Medicine in Germany and Italy

Medicine is one of the hardest subjects to get into in both countries, and the rules differ sharply. This file covers the top medical schools, the entrance exams, and the long road from admission to actually practising.

## Before you start

Two things shape every decision about studying medicine in Europe. First, the language: German medicine is taught mostly in German, while Italy runs a well-established set of English-taught medical degrees. Second, the competition: places are limited and capped in both countries, so admission turns on an entrance test or a high school grade, not just on submitting an application.

Medicine in continental Europe is usually a single long undergraduate degree (around six years) rather than a separate Bachelor's then medical school. You apply to it straight out of secondary school.

## Germany

German medicine has a strong international reputation, and the degree is respected everywhere. The trade-off is that it is highly competitive and mostly taught in German.

### Top medical schools

| University | Known for |
| --- | --- |
| Heidelberg University | Oldest medical faculty in Germany, major biomedical research cluster |
| Charité – Universitätsmedizin Berlin | One of Europe's largest university hospitals, strong research |
| LMU Munich | Large, classic medical faculty with a big teaching hospital |
| TU Munich (TUM) | Strong clinical and research medicine, Klinikum rechts der Isar |
| University of Freiburg | Well-regarded medicine and life sciences |
| University of Tübingen | Long medical tradition and research strength |
| University of Göttingen | Historic medical and scientific faculty |

### The competitive picture

German public medicine has very few places relative to demand, allocated through a national system. Admission has historically been dominated by the **Numerus Clausus (NC)** — effectively a grade cutoff so high that a near-perfect secondary-school grade is often needed. Alongside the grade, the **TMS (Test für Medizinische Studiengänge)**, an aptitude test, can substantially improve your chances at many universities. How the grade, the TMS, and other criteria combine is set by a national allocation framework and changes, so confirm the current rules rather than relying on older descriptions.

The central allocation for medicine runs through **hochschulstart.de**. For how the process works and what international applicants in particular need to do, start with [DAAD](https://www.daad.de) and the [Germany admissions guide](../germany/admissions.md).

### Language and the Studienkolleg

Because most German medical programs are taught in German, you'll usually need a high level of German (commonly C1, via TestDaF or DSH — confirm the level each faculty asks for). If your secondary-school qualification isn't recognised as equivalent to the German Abitur for direct entry, you may first need a **Studienkolleg** (a one-year foundation course) and its final exam, the Feststellungsprüfung. Whether you need one depends on your country and qualification; [uni-assist](https://www.uni-assist.de) and the university can tell you.

### How to apply

Read the specific faculty's page first, then expect some combination of: proof of German, a recognised secondary qualification (possibly via Studienkolleg), and the national allocation process for the place itself. Many international students go through [uni-assist](https://www.uni-assist.de). The exact route and deadlines vary by university and your status, so confirm on the official sources.

## Italy

Italy is the more accessible option for international students who want to study medicine **in English**, and it has well-known medical schools with long histories.

### Top medical schools

| University | Known for |
| --- | --- |
| University of Milan (and other Milan universities) | Several English-taught medicine programs, large clinical base |
| University of Padua | One of the oldest medical traditions in the world, strong research |
| University of Bologna | Oldest university in the Western world, respected medicine |
| University of Pavia | Long-running English-taught medicine (Harvey course) |
| Sapienza University of Rome | Very large, broad medical faculty |
| University of Turin, Naples, and others | Additional Italian- and some English-taught programs |

Italian and English-taught programs both exist. The English-taught medical degrees are the ones most international students target.

### The IMAT entrance exam

Admission to English-taught medicine in Italy runs largely through the **IMAT (International Medical Admissions Test)**, a multiple-choice exam covering logic, general knowledge, and science. Places are ranked by IMAT score against a national or per-university quota, including a quota for non-EU candidates.

The IMAT is administered in connection with **CISIA**; check the official test information at [cisia.it](https://www.cisia.it) and the national procedure for international students at [studyinitaly.esteri.it](https://studyinitaly.esteri.it). Test dates, registration, the exact syllabus, the score scaling, and which universities use it change year to year, so treat any specific figure you read elsewhere as something to verify on those official pages.

Italian-taught medicine generally uses its own national entrance test rather than the IMAT — confirm which test your target program requires.

### How to apply

For English-taught medicine the usual shape is: register and sit the IMAT, complete pre-enrollment on [Universitaly](https://www.universitaly.it) through your local Italian consulate, get your qualification recognised, and then take your place against the relevant quota if your IMAT rank is high enough. The full sequence, with the documents and the consulate step, is in the [Italy admissions guide](../italy/admissions.md). The national overview for international students is [studyinitaly.esteri.it](https://studyinitaly.esteri.it).

## Germany vs. Italy at a glance

| | Germany | Italy (English track) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Main language | Mostly German | English programs widely available |
| Entrance route | NC grade + TMS, national allocation | IMAT score + quota |
| Language proof | High German (e.g. C1), often Studienkolleg | English; Italian useful for clinical years |
| Where to confirm | [DAAD](https://www.daad.de), hochschulstart.de | [cisia.it](https://www.cisia.it), [studyinitaly.esteri.it](https://studyinitaly.esteri.it) |

This table is a starting orientation. Verify the current detail for your specific program before you act on it.

## How to choose

If you already have strong German or are willing to invest a year or two reaching it, Germany gives you a respected degree and study at a country with a large healthcare system, though entry is fiercely competitive on grades. If you want to study in English and your strength is the kind of science-and-logic exam the IMAT tests, Italy's English-taught programs are the more realistic route, and several of its medical schools are historic and well regarded.

A practical note for both: even in an English-taught program in Italy, you will need Italian for clinical rotations with real patients. Plan to learn the local language regardless of the teaching language, because medicine is practised in the local language.

## The long path to practising

Getting admitted is the start of a long process. After roughly six years of study you graduate, but practising medicine then involves licensing, registration, and usually specialty (residency) training, each with its own competitive entry. The rules for where you can practise depend on where you trained, your nationality, and where you want to work, and recognition of a foreign medical degree is its own procedure.

This handbook does not cover medical licensing in detail, because it is highly individual and changes. Use the official sources — your university, the national medical authority, and the immigration pages linked from the [Germany](../germany/admissions.md) and [Italy](../italy/admissions.md) guides — and confirm the current path before committing years to it.
