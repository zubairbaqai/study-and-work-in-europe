## Why these two countries for engineering

Engineering in Germany and northern Italy is tied directly to industry — automotive, machinery, electronics, aerospace, and manufacturing. That means strong technical universities, real industry links for internships and theses, and a job market that actively hires engineers. Many of the best programs at Master's level are taught in English, while most Bachelor's are in German or Italian.

## Germany

Germany's technical universities (TU) are the core of its engineering reputation.

### Top engineering schools

| University | Signature strengths |
| --- | --- |
| RWTH Aachen | The classic German engineering name: mechanical, electrical, materials, automotive/production |
| TU Munich (TUM) | Broad and top-rated across mechanical, electrical, aerospace, and more |
| Karlsruhe Institute of Technology (KIT) | Mechanical and electrical engineering, energy, plus a national research centre |
| University of Stuttgart | Mechanical and aerospace engineering, automotive, in Germany's car region (Porsche, Mercedes, Bosch) |
| TU Berlin | Strong engineering in a big-city tech and startup ecosystem |
| TU Darmstadt | Mechanical and electrical engineering, IT security, near the Frankfurt industrial belt |
| TU Dresden | Engineering and microelectronics, good value in eastern Germany |

### How to apply

For Master's programs, you usually apply either through [uni-assist](https://www.uni-assist.de) or directly to the university, depending on the school. Expect to submit a recognised Bachelor's in a related field, transcripts, proof of language (English for English-taught Master's, German otherwise), and often a statement of purpose. Engineering Master's frequently have specific prerequisite-module requirements, so read the program page closely. Deadlines and document lists are set per program and change, so confirm them on the university's site and via [DAAD](https://www.daad.de) and the [Germany admissions guide](../germany/admissions.md).

Bachelor's applicants from many countries need the secondary qualification recognised, and may need a Studienkolleg foundation year; [uni-assist](https://www.uni-assist.de) can tell you whether you do.

## Italy

Italy's engineering centre of gravity is its two big polytechnics in the north, plus several strong general universities.

### Top engineering schools

| University | Signature strengths |
| --- | --- |
| Politecnico di Milano | Italy's leading engineering and design school, broad and internationally ranked |
| Politecnico di Torino | Strong across mechanical, automotive (near FIAT/Stellantis), aerospace, electronics |
| University of Padua | Long engineering tradition, broad faculty |
| University of Pisa | Strong engineering and a major research environment |
| Sapienza University of Rome, University of Bologna | Large engineering faculties with English-taught options |

The two Politecnici (Milano and Torino) are the names most international engineering students start with, and both run a substantial set of English-taught Master's.

### How to apply

For English-taught Master's, you apply through the university's own admissions portal, and non-EU students applying from abroad also complete pre-enrollment on [Universitaly](https://www.universitaly.it) via the Italian consulate. You'll typically need a recognised Bachelor's, transcripts, English proof, and sometimes a portfolio of coursework or an evaluation of your background. Some programs assess applicants in rounds with their own deadlines. The full procedure, including the consulate and qualification-recognition steps, is in the [Italy admissions guide](../italy/admissions.md), and the national overview is [studyinitaly.esteri.it](https://studyinitaly.esteri.it). Confirm every deadline on the university's page, since they vary by program and intake.

## Comparing the top schools

| | Germany | Italy |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Flagship names | RWTH Aachen, TUM, KIT, Stuttgart | Politecnico di Milano, Politecnico di Torino |
| Industry near campus | Automotive south (Stuttgart, Munich), broad industry | Automotive (Torino), design/manufacturing (Milano) |
| English Master's | Widely available | Widely available, especially at the Politecnici |
| Apply via | [uni-assist](https://www.uni-assist.de) or direct | University portal + [Universitaly](https://www.universitaly.it) |

## How to choose

Match the school to the specialisation and the industry around it. For automotive and mechanical engineering, Stuttgart and Munich in Germany and Torino in Italy sit inside the relevant industrial regions. For broad, high-ranked engineering with strong international cohorts, TUM and RWTH in Germany and Politecnico di Milano in Italy are the usual shortlist. Then filter by which programs run in a language you can study in, and by which ones match your Bachelor's background closely enough to admit you.

For the full list of universities and their wider strengths, see [Germany universities](../germany/universities.md) and [Italy universities](../italy/universities.md).

## Job outlook

Engineering is one of the fields where both countries actively recruit international graduates. Germany has well-known shortages in engineering and a structured post-study route: an 18-month residence permit to find a job and the EU Blue Card for qualified roles — see [working in Germany](../germany/working-and-jobs.md). Northern Italy's manufacturing and automotive sector hires engineers too, and there's a study-to-work conversion route — see [working in Italy](../italy/working-and-jobs.md). Learning the local language widens your options considerably, since much of the hiring outside large multinationals happens in German or Italian.

## Preparing your application

You'll need a CV for internships, working-student roles, theses with companies, and graduate jobs:

- [Europass CV](../job-hunt/europass-cv.md) — the EU standard, often expected for scholarships and Italian applications.
- [ATS-friendly resumes](../job-hunt/ats-friendly-resume.md) — for the large employers who screen with software.
- [German & Italian CV norms](../job-hunt/application-cv-by-country.md) — how a German Lebenslauf differs from an Italian CV.
